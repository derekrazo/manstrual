### Week 1: The Complete Hormonal System

#### Lesson 1.1: The Four Hormones That Run Everything
**Learning Objectives:**
- Understand estrogen, progesterone, testosterone, and FSH/LH roles
- Map hormone fluctuations across 28 days
- Recognize cause-and-effect relationships between hormones and behavior

**Content:**
- **Estrogen (Estradiol)**: The "feel-good" hormone
  - Rises during follicular phase (days 1-14)
  - Peaks just before ovulation
  - Increases dopamine receptor sensitivity (motivation, reward)
  - Enhances serotonin production (mood stability)
  - Stimulates hippocampus (memory, learning)
  - **Partner Impact**: Higher energy, better mood, increased social engagement, enhanced verbal communication

- **Progesterone**: The "calming" hormone (with caveats)
  - Dominates luteal phase (days 15-28)
  - Metabolizes into allopregnanolone (affects GABA receptors)
  - Can be anxiolytic (calming) or anxiety-inducing depending on individual sensitivity
  - **Partner Impact**: Need for rest, introspection, potential irritability in sensitive individuals

- **Testosterone**: Often forgotten in women
  - Peaks at ovulation
  - Drives libido and assertiveness
  - Fluctuates throughout cycle
  - **Partner Impact**: Increased sexual desire mid-cycle, confidence peaks

- **FSH/LH**: The cycle regulators
  - FSH stimulates follicle development
  - LH surge triggers ovulation
  - The "behind the scenes" hormones

**Unique Insights from Research:**
- 208 out of 397 metabolites show significant rhythmicity across the cycle (Nature, 2018)
- Estrogen stimulates aromatase enzyme converting androstenedione to testosterone, then to estradiol
- Progesterone has CATABOLIC effects (breaks down energy stores) explaining luteal phase fatigue

**Practical Application:**
- Create a visual "hormone map" participants can reference
- "If she's doing X, here's what's likely happening hormonally"
- Debunk: "It's just PMS" - No, there are 4 distinct phases with different needs

#### Lesson 1.2: The Four Phases Decoded
**Learning Objectives:**
- Identify characteristics of each phase
- Understand day ranges and variability
- Recognize phase transitions

**Content:**

**Phase 1: Menstrual Phase (Days 1-5)**
- **Hormones**: All hormones at baseline/low
- **Biology**: Uterine lining shedding, prostaglandin release (causes cramps)
- **Energy**: Lowest point of cycle
- **Mood**: Can be relief (from PMS ending) or low due to blood loss/pain
- **Brain**: Baseline connectivity, fresh start
- **What She Needs**: Rest, warmth, pain management, low-demand activities
- **What NOT to do**: Plan intense activities, expect high energy, minimize her pain

**Phase 2: Follicular Phase (Days 6-14)**
- **Hormones**: Estrogen rising steadily
- **Biology**: Follicle maturation, endometrium rebuilding
- **Energy**: Increasing, peaks at end of phase
- **Mood**: Optimistic, outgoing, confident
- **Brain**: Enhanced verbal fluency, increased neural connectivity, improved memory
- **Cognitive Function**: Peak performance in communication and learning tasks
- **What She Needs**: Engagement, new experiences, social activities
- **Partner Opportunity**: Plan dates, have important conversations, introduce new ideas

**Phase 3: Ovulation (Days 14-17)**
- **Hormones**: Estrogen and testosterone PEAK, LH surge
- **Biology**: Egg release, peak fertility window
- **Energy**: Highest of entire cycle
- **Mood**: Confident, social, attractive (to self and others)
- **Libido**: PEAK (testosterone + fertility signals)
- **Brain**: Maximum neural network flexibility and adaptability
- **Social**: Most outgoing, best at reading social cues
- **What She Needs**: Sexual intimacy, social engagement, feeling desired
- **Evolutionary Note**: Research shows women prefer more masculine faces, symmetry, and deeper voices during this window

**Phase 4: Luteal Phase (Days 18-28)**
- **Hormones**: Progesterone dominant, estrogen second peak then drop
- **Biology**: Endometrium thickening, preparing for potential pregnancy
- **Energy**: High early (days 18-21), declining mid-late (days 22-28)
- **Mood**: BIFURCATED
  - Early luteal: Still good, stable
  - Mid-late luteal (PMS): Irritability, anxiety, emotional sensitivity
- **Brain**: Heightened amygdala reactivity (emotional processing), reduced prefrontal control
- **Sensitivity**: To rejection, criticism, conflict - HIGHEST in late luteal
- **What She Needs**:
  - Early: Normal partnership
  - Late: Patience, validation, space, anticipatory support
- **PMS Window**: Days 24-28 typically (but varies individually)

**Critical Understanding:**
- Average cycle is 28 days, but 24-35 days is normal
- Phase lengths vary between individuals
- Tracking is essential (don't assume)

#### Lesson 1.3: Neuroscience of the Cycling Brain
**Learning Objectives:**
- Understand brain regions affected by hormonal fluctuations
- Recognize why "she's being emotional" is neurologically real (and not dismissive)
- Connect brain changes to behavior changes

**Content:**

**Brain Regions Undergoing Neuroplastic Changes:**

1. **Hippocampus (Memory & Learning)**
   - Responds to estrogen levels
   - Volume changes across cycle
   - Follicular phase: Enhanced memory consolidation
   - Practical implication: She may remember details better/worse depending on phase

2. **Amygdala (Emotional Processing & Fear)**
   - LEFT amygdala more active in luteal phase
   - RIGHT amygdala more active in follicular phase
   - Increased reactivity during luteal phase
   - Practical implication: Emotional triggers are LOUDER during PMS neurologically, not "overreacting"

3. **Prefrontal Cortex (Executive Function, Impulse Control)**
   - Modulated by estrogen and progesterone
   - Reduced inhibitory control during luteal phase
   - Practical implication: Harder to "bite her tongue" during PMS - reduced filtering

4. **Striatum (Reward Processing)**
   - Dopamine sensitivity varies with estrogen
   - Follicular: Enhanced reward response
   - Luteal: Blunted reward response
   - Practical implication: Same gesture may land differently depending on phase

**Neurotransmitter Systems:**

1. **Serotonin (Mood, Anxiety, Impulse Control)**
   - Estrogen increases serotonin production and receptor sensitivity
   - Luteal phase drop in estrogen = serotonin drop
   - PMDD strongly linked to serotonergic dysfunction
   - Clinical finding: SSRIs (serotonin medications) highly effective for PMS/PMDD

2. **Dopamine (Motivation, Reward, Pleasure)**
   - Peaks at ovulation
   - Estrogen enhances dopamine receptor sensitivity
   - Luteal decline contributes to anhedonia (reduced pleasure)

3. **GABA (Calming, Anti-Anxiety)**
   - Progesterone metabolite (allopregnanolone) affects GABA-A receptors
   - Should be calming, but paradoxical anxiety in PMDD individuals
   - Individual variability is HUGE

**2024 Neuroimaging Breakthroughs:**
- Nature Neuroscience study: Widespread brain volume changes correlate with progesterone levels
- Neural network complexity changes dramatically across cycle
- This is NOT "all in her head" - it's measurable brain reorganization

**Teaching Approach:**
- Use brain scan visuals showing actual differences
- Frame as "her brain is literally different this week" (validating)
- Emphasize: She's not choosing to be emotional; her amygdala is firing 40% hotter

### Week 3: Mastering Each Phase

#### Lesson 3.1: Menstrual Phase (Days 1-5) - The Reset

**Core Principle**: Comfort without smothering, support without fixing

**Biology Recap:**
- Prostaglandin release causing cramps (similar to inflammation response)
- Iron loss from bleeding
- Energy at lowest point
- Pain ranges from mild to debilitating (endometriosis can be excruciating)

**What She's Experiencing:**
- Physical: Cramps, bloating, fatigue, headaches, lower back pain
- Emotional: Relief (PMS is over!), vulnerability, need for gentleness
- Social: Desire for low-key connection or solitude
- Energy: 30-50% of normal

**The 3 Pillars of Menstrual Support:**

**1. Pain Management Partnership**
- Ask (don't assume): "What usually helps your cramps?"
- Offer options: "Would heat, massage, or meds help right now?"
- Stock supplies:
  - Ibuprofen/Naproxen (anti-inflammatories reduce prostaglandins)
  - Heating pad (keep charged/ready)
  - Her preferred menstrual products
- **NEVER**: Minimize pain, compare to other women, suggest "it's not that bad"

**2. Anticipatory Task Relief**
- Take tasks OFF her plate, don't add to it
- Examples:
  - Meal prep/cooking
  - Grocery shopping
  - Cleaning (especially bathroom)
  - Childcare duties
  - Errands
- **Do it without being asked** (use cycle tracking)

**3. Emotional Presence Without Pressure**
- Be available but not hovering
- Offer: "I'm here if you need anything, otherwise I'll give you space"
- Physical comfort: Gentle touch, back rubs, cuddling (if wanted)
- **Avoid**: Planning intense activities, expecting enthusiasm

**Communication Scripts:**
- "I noticed you're on day 1. I'm taking dinner tonight. Any cravings?"
- "Want me to grab extra pads/tampons while I'm out?"
- "Movie night on the couch? Or would you rather have some alone time?"

**Sexual Intimacy:**
- NEVER assume she doesn't want sex
- Some women have increased libido (pelvic congestion relief)
- Ask openly: "How are you feeling about intimacy right now?"
- Period sex is healthy and normal (if both partners comfortable)
- Offer alternatives: massage, non-penetrative intimacy

**Red Flags Requiring Medical Attention:**
- Soaking through pad/tampon every hour
- Pain not responding to OTC medication
- Fever during period
- Severe pain interfering with daily life (possible endometriosis)
- **Your role**: Encourage seeing doctor, offer to attend appointment

#### Lesson 3.4: Luteal Phase (Days 18-28) - The Storm

**Core Principle**: Validate, don't fix. Support, don't solve.

**This phase requires TWO distinct approaches:**
- Early Luteal (Days 18-22): Relatively stable
- Late Luteal/PMS (Days 23-28): High-sensitivity mode

**Biology Recap:**
- Progesterone dominant
- Estrogen has second smaller peak then DROPS
- Serotonin declining
- Amygdala reactivity increasing
- Prefrontal cortex regulation decreasing

- Physical: Bloating, breast tenderness, headaches, fatigue, food cravings, sleep disruption
- Emotional: Irritability, anxiety, sadness, feeling overwhelmed
- Cognitive: Brain fog, distractibility, negativity bias
- Social: Desire for withdrawal, reduced tolerance for bullshit
- Sensory: Heightened sensitivity to noise, light, smells

**The 5 Pillars of PMS Mastery:**

**1. Anticipatory Support**
- Use cycle tracking to PREPARE
- Week before expected period:
  - Stock chocolate, comfort foods
  - Clear your schedule for more presence
  - Take on extra tasks proactively
  - Plan low-key weekends
- "I've got dinner/cleanup/kids this week, you focus on you"

**2. Emotional Validation (NOT Problem-Solving)**
- THE CARDINAL RULE: Do not try to fix her feelings
- When she vents:
  - LISTEN without solutions
  - Validate: "That sounds really frustrating"
  - Empathize: "I can see why you'd feel that way"
  - Ask: "What would help right now?"
- **NEVER**: "You're being too sensitive," "Calm down," "Is it your period?"

- Recognize: 80% of relationship conflicts occur in luteal phase (research)
- She's not picking fights intentionally - threshold is lower
- **De-escalation script**:
  - "I can see you're really upset. This conversation is important. Can we press pause for 20 minutes so I can give you my full attention?"
  - Take a break if flooding occurs (20+ minutes)
  - Return with empathy, not defensiveness
- **Avoid**: Escalating, logic-battling, dismissing

**4. Physical Comfort**
- Offer (don't impose):
  - Back/shoulder massage
  - Heating pad for abdomen
  - Warm bath (offer to run it)
  - Comfortable clothes (your softest sweatshirt)
- Respect: If she wants space, give it
- Food: Ask about cravings, have snacks ready

**5. Lower Expectations (on her AND you)**
- This is NOT the week for:
  - Deep relationship talks
  - Family events requiring her to perform
  - Starting new projects
  - Critique or feedback
- This IS the week for:
  - Netflix and actual chill
  - Comfort food
  - Low-demand presence
  - Grace and patience

**Communication During PMS:**

**The 3-Second Rule Before Speaking:**
Ask yourself:
1. Is this necessary to say RIGHT NOW?
2. Is this kind?
3. Would I say this to my boss?

**Safe Phrases:**
- "You're not crazy. This is real."
- "What do you need from me?"
- "I'm here."
- "Take all the time you need."
- "I love you, and this will pass."

**Forbidden Phrases:**
- "Are you on your period?" (NEVER)
- "You're overreacting"
- "Calm down"
- "Other women don't get this upset"
- "It's just hormones" (dismissive)
- "You're being irrational"

- Libido typically LOW (but not always)
- If she initiates, follow her lead
- Orgasm can relieve cramps (endorphins)
- Gentle, connection-focused rather than performance
- Non-sexual touch often more appreciated

- 5-8% of women have premenstrual dysphoric disorder
- Symptoms are debilitating, not just annoying
- May include: severe depression, suicidal ideation, rage, panic attacks
- **Medical intervention needed**: SSRIs, therapy, hormonal treatments
- **Your role**:
  - Encourage professional help
  - Track symptoms to show doctor
  - Attend appointments if welcomed
  - Crisis support if needed

### Week 4: Supporting Chronic Conditions

#### Lesson 4.1: PMDD - When PMS Is Debilitating

**Learning Objectives:**
- Distinguish between PMS and PMDD
- Understand the severity of PMDD symptoms
- Learn partner support strategies for severe symptoms
- Know when professional help is critical

**Content:**

**What is PMDD?**
- Premenstrual Dysphoric Disorder
- Affects 5-8% of menstruating women
- Recognized psychiatric condition (DSM-5)
- Symptoms severe enough to impair daily functioning
- NOT "bad PMS" - qualitatively different experience

**Diagnostic Criteria (simplified):**
Requires 5+ of these symptoms in the week before menstruation:
- Marked mood swings
- Marked irritability or anger
- Marked depressed mood, hopelessness
- Marked anxiety, tension
- Decreased interest in usual activities
- Difficulty concentrating
- Fatigue, low energy
- Appetite changes or cravings
- Sleep changes
- Feeling overwhelmed or out of control
- Physical symptoms (breast tenderness, bloating, pain)

**Critical: Symptoms must resolve within days of menstruation starting**

**The PMDD Experience (from research and patient reports):**
- "I become a different person for 10 days a month"
- Rage episodes that feel uncontrollable
- Suicidal ideation (in severe cases)
- Complete loss of ability to cope with normal stress
- Relationship-threatening conflicts
- Inability to work effectively
- Physical pain combined with mental anguish

**Causes:**
- NOT just "too much progesterone"
- Abnormal response to NORMAL hormone fluctuations
- Serotonergic dysfunction
- GABA-A receptor sensitivity to allopregnanolone (progesterone metabolite)
- Genetic component (tends to run in families)
- Inflammatory response differences

**Treatment Options (Medical):**
- **SSRIs** (selective serotonin reuptake inhibitors) - FIRST LINE
  - Can be taken continuously or luteal-phase only
  - 60-75% response rate
  - Examples: fluoxetine (Prozac), sertraline (Zoloft)
- **Hormonal contraception** (continuous use to eliminate cycle)
- **GnRH agonists** (severe cases - induces medical menopause)
- **Cognitive Behavioral Therapy** (CBT)
- **Lifestyle**: exercise, stress reduction, sleep hygiene

**Partner Support Strategies:**

**1. Tracking and Prediction**
- Use app or calendar to predict symptom onset
- Know the 10-day danger zone
- Clear your schedule for extra presence
- Warn her: "Hey, based on tracking, this might be a hard week. I'm prepared to support you."

**2. Crisis Planning**
- During good week, create together:
  - Warning signs she's spiraling
  - What helps vs. what doesn't
  - Emergency contacts
  - When to seek medical help
- Example: "If I say I want to hurt myself, take me to ER"

**3. Holding Space for Dark Emotions**
- She may say things like "I hate my life" or "I want to disappear"
- Remember: This is PMDD talking, not her true self
- Validate without panic: "I know this feels unbearable right now. It will pass. You're safe."
- Do NOT: Argue with her feelings, minimize, or take personally

**4. Radical Acceptance**
- Some months will be bad no matter what you do
- Your job is presence, not cure
- Lower ALL expectations during symptomatic phase
- Celebrate the good weeks

**5. Encourage Professional Help**
- Many women suffer for years without treatment
- SSRIs can be life-changing
- Offer to help find doctors, attend appointments
- Research shows: supportive partners reduce symptom severity

**Communication Scripts:**

*When she's in PMDD rage:*
- "I hear that you're furious. I'm not going anywhere. When you're ready to talk, I'm here."
- (Then give space unless she asks for presence)

*When she's in PMDD depression:*
- "I know everything feels hopeless right now. This is the PMDD. The real you is still in there, and she'll be back in a few days."

*When she's apologizing after an episode:*
- "You don't need to apologize. I know that wasn't you. I love all of you, including the hard weeks."

**What NOT to Do:**
- Don't fight back during rage episodes
- Don't take her words literally (they're not rational)
- Don't abandon her during worst symptoms
- Don't suggest "just positive thinking"
- Don't make major relationship decisions during PMDD week

**Red Flags - Get Help Immediately:**
- Suicidal statements or plans
- Self-harm behavior
- Inability to function (can't get out of bed, go to work)
- Violent behavior toward others
- Symptoms not improving after menses start

**Resources to Provide:**
- International Association for Premenstrual Disorders (IAPMD)
- PMDD-specialized therapists
- Support groups (online and local)
- Medical providers experienced with PMDD

#### Lesson 4.2: PCOS - Polycystic Ovary Syndrome

**Learning Objectives:**
- Understand PCOS symptoms and impacts
- Support partner with PCOS-related challenges
- Navigate irregular cycles
- Address body image and fertility concerns

**Content:**

**What is PCOS?**
- Affects 8-13% of reproductive-age women
- Hormonal disorder affecting ovaries
- Leading cause of infertility
- Metabolic implications (insulin resistance, diabetes risk)

**Key Characteristics:**
- Irregular or absent periods
- Excess androgens (male hormones) â†’ acne, facial hair, hair loss
- Polycystic ovaries (multiple small follicles)
- Insulin resistance and weight gain
- Diagnosis requires 2 of 3: irregular periods, hyperandrogenism, polycystic ovaries

**Physical Symptoms:**
- Irregular cycles (35+ days between periods, or very long periods)
- Hirsutism (excess body/facial hair)
- Acne
- Male-pattern baldness or hair thinning
- Weight gain, difficulty losing weight
- Darkening skin (acanthosis nigricans)
- Skin tags

**Emotional/Psychological Impact:**
- Body image struggles (hirsutism, weight, acne)
- Depression and anxiety (2-3x higher rates)
- Fertility anxiety
- Social stigma
- Feeling "less feminine"

**Relationship Impacts (from research):**
- Sexual self-consciousness
- Reduced sexual desire (related to body image)
- Fertility concerns strain relationship
- Need for partner validation

**Treatment:**
- Hormonal birth control (regulate cycles, reduce androgens)
- Metformin (insulin resistance)

*Nutritional needs:*

- **Magnesium** (PMS symptom reduction): Nuts, seeds, dark chocolate
- **Calcium** (reduces PMS): Dairy, fortified plant milk, leafy greens

- Progesterone increases appetite
- Blood sugar swings worsen mood
- Honor cravings in moderation
- Offer healthier alternatives: dark chocolate vs. candy bars

*Practical support:*
- Stock comfort foods (healthy versions)
- Meal prep to reduce decision fatigue
- Don't judge cravings or eating patterns

**Evidence-Based Supplements for PMS/PMDD:**

**Calcium:**
- **Dose**: 1000-1200 mg/day
- **Evidence**: STRONGEST evidence for PMS reduction (large RCT)
- **Effects**: Reduces mood symptoms, pain, bloating, cravings
- **Research**: 48% reduction in PMS symptoms in some studies

**Magnesium:**
- **Dose**: 200-400 mg/day
- **Evidence**: Moderate evidence
- **Effects**: Reduces water retention, mood symptoms, cramps
- **Best form**: Magnesium glycinate (better absorption, less laxative effect)
- **Timing**: Take in luteal phase or continuously

**Vitamin B6:**
- **Dose**: 50-100 mg/day (max 100 mg to avoid neuropathy)
- **Evidence**: Mixed results, some benefit
- **Effects**: May reduce mood symptoms, breast tenderness
- **Combination**: Works better with magnesium

**Vitamin D:**
- **Dose**: 1000-2000 IU/day (based on blood levels)
- **Evidence**: Deficiency worsens PMS
- **Effects**: Mood support, pain reduction

**Omega-3 Fatty Acids (Fish Oil):**
- **Dose**: 1000-2000 mg EPA+DHA daily
- **Evidence**: Moderate evidence for PMDD
- **Effects**: Anti-inflammatory, mood stabilization, cramp reduction

**Chasteberry (Vitex):**
- **Dose**: 20-40 mg daily
- **Evidence**: European studies show benefit for PMS
- **Effects**: May regulate progesterone, reduce breast pain
- **Caution**: Can interact with hormonal BC

**Evening Primrose Oil:**
- **Evidence**: Weak (studies inconsistent)
- **Common use**: Breast tenderness
- **Note**: Not strongly recommended but low risk

1. **Research together**: Review evidence, consult doctor
2. **Practical support**:

   - Set up pill organizer
   - Reminder to take daily
3. **Track effects**: "How are you feeling this month compared to last?"
4. **Manage expectations**: Supplements take 2-3 cycles to show effect
5. **Medical check**: Encourage doctor consultation (especially for PMDD)

#### Lesson 8.2: Contraception, Fertility & Family Planning

**Learning Objectives:**
- Understand how contraception affects the cycle and mood
- Navigate family planning conversations
- Support fertility journeys (conception or prevention)
- Recognize when cycle changes signal medical issues

**Content:**

**Hormonal Contraception & The Cycle:**

**Important Reality:**
- Hormonal birth control SUPPRESSES the natural cycle
- No true follicular, ovulation, or luteal phases on the pill
- The "period" is withdrawal bleeding, not true menstruation
- Hormone levels artificially flat instead of cycling

**Types of Hormonal Contraception:**

**1. Combined Oral Contraceptives (The Pill):**
- Contains synthetic estrogen + progestin
- Suppresses ovulation
- Creates artificial stable hormone state
- "Period" during placebo week is not real period

*Effects on mood:*
- 43.6% of users report mood changes
- 4-10% experience significant mood side effects
- Women with history of depression MORE at risk
- Older pills with ethinyl estradiol linked to more mood issues

*Effects on desire:*
- Can DECREASE libido (common complaint)
- Reduces testosterone production
- Flattens desire across month (no ovulation peak)

**2. Progestin-Only Pills (Mini-Pill):**
- No estrogen, only synthetic progesterone
- May or may not suppress ovulation
- Greater propensity for depressive disorders in vulnerable women

**3. Hormonal IUDs (Mirena, Kyleena, Skyla):**
- Localized progestin in uterus
- May still ovulate (lighter systemic effects)
- Periods may stop entirely
- Fewer mood effects than pill (for most)

**4. Implant/Shot (Nexplanon, Depo-Provera):**
- Progestin-only
- Suppress ovulation
- Side effects similar to progestin pills
- Depo associated with depression risk

**Partner Considerations When She's On Hormonal BC:**

1. **No Natural Cycle**: The modules in this course describe NATURAL cycles. If she's on hormonal BC, she won't experience these fluctuations.

2. **Mood Effects**: If she's experiencing mood issues, birth control may be culprit
   - Encourage discussion with doctor
   - May need to switch methods or stop hormonal BC

3. **Libido Impact**: If libido is persistently low, BC might be the cause
   - Supportive conversation: "I've read BC can affect desire. How do you feel about that?"

4. **Cycle Tracking Still Relevant**: When she STOPS BC, natural cycle returns (may take months to regulate)

**Non-Hormonal Options:**

**1. Copper IUD (Paragard):**
- No hormones, natural cycle intact
- Heavier, more painful periods (common side effect)
- All cycle patterns in this course apply

**2. Barrier Methods (Condoms, Diaphragm):**
- No hormonal effects
- Natural cycle intact
- Requires use-time compliance

**3. Fertility Awareness Methods (FAM):**
- Tracking ovulation to avoid fertile days
- Requires commitment from both partners
- 99% effective if done perfectly, ~76% with typical use
- Natural cycle intact and deeply understood

**Fertility & Family Planning:**

**When Trying to Conceive:**

*Cycle knowledge is ESSENTIAL:*
- Ovulation window = 5 days before to 1 day after ovulation
- Track with BBT, OPKs, cervical mucus
- Stress reduces fertility (stay connected, not transactional)

*Challenges:*
- Average time to conceive: 6 months to 1 year (normal)
- Performance pressure
- Monthly disappointment when period arrives
- Relationship strain

*Partner support:*
- Share the tracking responsibility
- Maintain intimacy outside fertile window
- Support through monthly cycles of hope/grief
- Know when to seek fertility specialist (12 months if under 35, 6 months if over 35)

**When Preventing Pregnancy:**

*Shared responsibility:*
- Don't put all burden on her (hormonal side effects)
- Consider vasectomy (if family complete)
- Use condoms to share load
- Support her method choice

**Menopause Transition (Perimenopause):**

*For partners of women 40+:*
- Perimenopause can start in 40s
- Irregular cycles, hot flashes, mood changes, sleep disruption
- Can last 4-10 years
- Similar support principles: validation, patience, medical support
